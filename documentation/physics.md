## Angular Calculations

Before calculating physical forces, the system determines the geometric relationship between the wind, the boat, and the sail. Angles are measured in degrees from **0°** to **360°**.

**Relative Wind Angle ($\theta_{rel}$)**
This represents the absolute shortest angle between the wind direction ($\theta_{wind}$) and the boat's heading ($\theta_{boat}$). This value is always bound between **0°** (wind dead ahead) and **180°** (wind dead astern).

$$\theta_{rel} = |(\theta_{wind} - \theta_{boat} + 180) \bmod 360 - 180|$$

**Optimal Sail Angle ($\theta_{opt}$)**
For maximum efficiency, the sail should bisect the angle between the wind and the boat's centerline.

$$\theta_{opt} = \frac{\theta_{rel}}{2}$$

**Sail Delta ($\Delta_{sail}$)**
This calculates the absolute difference between the actual sail angle ($\theta_{sail}$) and the ideal optimal sail angle. This value determines the efficiency of the aerodynamic lift.

$$\Delta_{sail} = | \theta_{sail} - \theta_{opt} |$$

## Forces and Movement

The boat's acceleration is governed by the forward drive generated by the sail, countered dynamically by the drag of the water.

**Forward Drive ($F_{drive}$)**
The driving force behaves differently depending on whether the boat is caught in the "No-Go Zone" (pointing **< 45°** into the wind).

- **In Irons ($< 45^\circ$):** The sail generates no aerodynamic lift. Instead, the wind pushes the physical hull ($W_{hull}$) and the exposed sail area ($S_{size}$) directly backward.
- **Sailing ($\ge 45^\circ$):** The sail generates forward drive proportional to the wind strength ($W_{strength}$), sail size ($S_{size}$), and sail trim accuracy. If $\Delta_{sail}$ exceeds **45°**, the drive drops to **0**.

$$
F_{drive} =
\begin{cases}
-W_{strength} \times (W_{hull} + S_{size}) \times \cos(\theta_{rel}) & \text{if } \theta_{rel} < 45 \\
W_{strength} \times S_{size} \times \max\left(0, 1 - \frac{\Delta_{sail}}{45}\right) & \text{if } \theta_{rel} \ge 45
\end{cases}
$$

**Drag Force ($F_{drag}$)**
Water drag scales quadratically with the boat's current velocity ($V_{current}$) and is tuned via a constant drag coefficient ($C_{drag}$). We multiply by the sign of the velocity to ensure drag always opposes the current direction of travel (whether moving forward or drifting backward). This organically enforces a terminal velocity.

$$F_{drag} = C_{drag} \times V_{current}^2 \times \text{sign}(V_{current})$$

**Velocity Update ($V_{new}$)**
At each step, the new velocity is simply the current velocity plus the net forces. (Assuming a standard boat mass of **1**).

$$V_{new} = V_{current} + F_{drive} - F_{drag}$$

## Failure States (Directional Capsizing)

To prevent physics exploits and teach the model to respect dangerous wind conditions, the boat will capsize if structural stress exceeds a maximum threshold ($F_{max}$).

The actual danger to the boat depends heavily on the wind's angle. Wind from behind pushes the boat safely forward, while wind on the beam (side-on) or head-on forces the boat to tip over.

**Raw Rig Force ($F_{rig}$)**
The raw force hitting the mast and sail assembly.

$$F_{rig} = W_{strength} \times S_{size} \times \cos(\Delta_{sail})$$

**Directional Stress ($F_{stress}$)**
A multiplier is applied based on the relative wind angle: **1.0** for head-on, **0.5** for side-on, and **0.0** for wind directly behind.

$$F_{stress} = F_{rig} \times \left( \frac{1 + \cos(\theta_{rel})}{2} \right)$$

If $F_{stress}$ exceeds $F_{max}$, a terminal capsize event is triggered, instantly ending the episode.

## Action Processing and Rate Limiting

To ensure the simulation remains physically grounded, the boat cannot instantly snap to new angles or sail sizes. The neural network controller will output its _absolute target_ values, and the physics engine will calculate the difference, limiting the change to strict maximum increments per frame.

**The Clamping Function**
We use a standard mathematical clamp to restrict the calculated step to our maximum mechanical limits.

$$\text{clamp}(x, \text{min}, \text{max}) = \max(\text{min}, \min(x, \text{max}))$$

**1. Boat Turn (Rudder) Target**
The controller requests a target heading ($\theta_{target}$). Because heading is circular, we first calculate the shortest rotational difference ($\text{diff}_{boat}$). We then clamp this difference by the maximum turn rate per frame ($R_{max}$) to get the actual step ($\Delta_{boat}$).

$$\text{diff}_{boat} = (\theta_{target} - \theta_{boat} + 180) \bmod 360 - 180$$
$$\Delta_{boat} = \text{clamp}(\text{diff}_{boat}, -R_{max}, R_{max})$$
$$\theta_{boat}^{new} = (\theta_{boat} + \Delta_{boat}) \bmod 360$$

**2. Sail Angle Target**
The controller requests a target sail angle ($\theta_{sail\_target}$). We calculate the linear difference, clamp it by the sail's maximum rotation rate ($A_{max}$), and finally ensure the resulting angle cannot exceed physical hull boundaries (e.g., restricted between **-90°** and **90°**).

$$\text{diff}_{sail} = \theta_{sail\_target} - \theta_{sail}$$
$$\Delta_{sail} = \text{clamp}(\text{diff}_{sail}, -A_{max}, A_{max})$$
$$\theta_{sail}^{new} = \text{clamp}(\theta_{sail} + \Delta_{sail}, -90, 90)$$

**3. Sail Size Target**
The controller requests a target sail size ($S_{target}$). We calculate the difference, clamp it by the hoisting rate limit ($S_{rate}$), and hard-cap the final size between its physical minimum ($S_{min}$) and maximum ($S_{max\_size}$) pixel lengths.

$$\text{diff}_{size} = S_{target} - S_{size}$$
$$\Delta_{size} = \text{clamp}(\text{diff}_{size}, -S_{rate}, S_{rate})$$
$$S_{size}^{new} = \text{clamp}(S_{size} + \Delta_{size}, S_{min}, S_{max\_size})$$

## Spatial Movement

After calculating the new velocity, the engine must translate this momentum into physical movement across the 2D grid. Assuming **0°** is North (pointing toward the objective at the top of the map), we use basic trigonometry to update the boat's $X$ and $Y$ coordinates.

Since the boat's heading ($\theta_{boat}$) is in degrees, it must first be converted to radians. Note that in navigation where **0°** is North (rather than East), sine calculates the X-axis (East/West) and cosine calculates the Y-axis (North/South).

$$\Delta X = V_{new} \times \sin(\text{radians}(\theta_{boat}))$$
$$\Delta Y = V_{new} \times \cos(\text{radians}(\theta_{boat}))$$

**Position Update**
The new coordinates are simply the current coordinates plus the calculated deltas.

$$X_{new} = X_{current} + \Delta X$$
$$Y_{new} = Y_{current} + \Delta Y$$

## Boundaries and Terminal States

To train the AI effectively, the environment distinguishes between different types of episode-ending (terminal) states. This allows the reward function to penalize physics mismanagement (capsizing) differently than navigation errors (going out of bounds), while rewarding successful navigation.

The map is defined as a fixed coordinate plane where $X \in [0, 256]$ and $Y \in [0, 256]$.

**1. Capsized (Physics Failure)**
Triggered internally by the physics engine if the directional stress on the hull exceeds the maximum threshold ($F_{max}$).

**2. Out of Bounds (Navigation Failure)**
Triggered if the boat's coordinates leave the playable grid in an unauthorized zone.

- $X < 0$ or $X > 256$
- $Y < 0$
- $Y \ge 256$ (If outside the target objective zone)

**3. Success (Objective Reached)**
Triggered if the boat successfully crosses the northern edge of the map within the defined target zone.

- $Y \ge 256$ AND $64 \le X \le 192$
